Image Classification & Bias

Understanding why bias exists in facial analysis datasets

Classifying images based on George W. [October 2018; individual project]

Question: How well does an facial classifier trained on George W Bush's face classify my facial features?

Solution: Trained an extra trees regressor model on 524 instances of George W Bush photographs.

Impact: Showed extremely low accuracy when applied to images of my face due to overtly biased dataset

Project Details

Key Insights

  1. Easy data does not mean unbiased data
  2. It can be difficult to accurately understand bias based on results of a training model, especially when there are dozens of granular features

Measurable Impact

  1. Built Jupyter notebook containing trained model
  2. Completed further secondary research on bias in data and potential avenues to mitigate

Justification

Biased Datasets

Labeled datasets are valuable and extremely useful in machine learning. However, as we have learned from previous experiences and opinions , there are significant risks to labeled datasets that are not representative of a diverse enough audience. The dataset "Labeled Faces in the Wild" falls exactly under this category. George W Bush is the face most common in this dataset because it is based on people who were on Yahoo News over a 2 year period in the early 2000s. While it is one of the more robust labeled facial datasets available, the fact that it is representing people were represented frequently in the news (e.g. the President of the United States) has implications for what models trained on this dataset can and cannot recognize. We could easily and naively arrive at horrifying and insensitive results that perpetuate societal biases and stereotypes.
